Identifying a butterfly species involves a hierarchical process of elimination. You start by determining the family, then move through the subfamily, tribe, and genus until you finally reach the specific species. This is achieved through the use of taxonomic identification keys.

The Structure of the Key

The keys consist of a series of numbered, paired statements. These pairs present contrasting physical characteristics (such as wing venation, antenna shape, or leg structure).

How to Use the Key

To identify a specimen, follow these logical steps:

Read the First Statement: Start at statement #1 of the relevant key.
Make a Decision:
If the statement is TRUE for your specimen: Move directly to the next statement immediately below it (e.g., from #1 to #2).
If the statement is FALSE for your specimen: Look at the number provided in parentheses at the end of that line. Jump to that specific statement number further down the key.
Continue Drilling Down: Repeat this process of selecting the true characteristic and jumping to the next step as directed.
Reach the Identification: Continue until the path leads you to a name (in bold italics), which identifies the butterfly’s group or species.
Example Walkthrough

Imagine you are identifying a butterfly using the "Key to the families." * Step 1: You look at statement #1. It is true. You "drop down" to statement #2. * Step 2: Statement #2 describes a characteristic your specimen lacks. You look at the number in parentheses (for example, #7). * Step 3: You jump to statement #7. If this is true, the text will identify the family (e.g., Lycaenidae).

Diagnostic Importance

The characteristics within the keys are listed in order of diagnostic importance. The first character mentioned is usually the primary structural difference (like a specific vein branch), while subsequent characters are supplementary aids, such as color or size.

Using Measurements as an Aid

Because many species look nearly identical, the keys often include forewing length (fwl). This is measured in millimeters from the base of the wing to the apex. This measurement helps distinguish between "sibling species" that share the same patterns but differ in scale.

Visual Verification

Once a name is reached, you can confirm the identification by referring to the provided plates. These illustrations show both the upperside and underside of males and females.
